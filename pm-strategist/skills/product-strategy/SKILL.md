# Product Strategy

## What Strategy IS and IS NOT

**Strategy is a coherent set of choices that creates focus.** It answers: where will we play, how will we win there, and what must be true for this to work.

Strategy is NOT:
- A list of goals ("grow 40% YoY" is an aspiration, not a strategy)
- A list of initiatives ("launch mobile app, expand to Europe" is a roadmap, not a strategy)
- A vision statement (vision is the destination; strategy is the route)
- A description of what you do (that's your product, not your strategy)

**The test**: Can someone on your team use this document to make a decision you didn't anticipate? If yes, it's a strategy. If no, it's a wish list.

## The Playing to Win Cascade

Before filling any canvas, internalize this cascade (A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin). Every element must reinforce the others. If any element contradicts another, the strategy is broken.

### 1. Winning Aspiration
What does winning look like? Not "be successful" — a specific, measurable end state.
- **Weak**: "Be the leading project management tool"
- **Strong**: "Within 3 years, be the default workflow tool for product teams at companies with 50-500 employees, measured by >40% market share in that segment"

### 2. Where to Play
Which specific segments, geographies, channels, product categories, and customer stages will you compete in?
- **Weak**: "We serve businesses of all sizes"
- **Strong**: "Series A-C B2B SaaS companies in North America, specifically the product and engineering teams, acquired through PLG with content marketing"

Choosing where to play means choosing where NOT to play. List both.

### 3. How to Win
What is your theory of competitive advantage in your chosen arena?
- **Cost leadership**: You deliver comparable value at lower cost (Walmart, Ryanair)
- **Differentiation**: You deliver unique value that justifies premium pricing (Apple, Salesforce)
- **Focus**: You serve a narrow segment better than anyone (Shopify for SMB e-commerce)

You must choose. "A little of both" is not a strategy — it's stuck in the middle.

### 4. Capabilities Required
What must you be world-class at to execute this strategy? These are the 3-5 capabilities that, together, enable your how-to-win.
- Not a generic list ("engineering, design, marketing")
- Specific to your strategy: "Real-time data pipeline that processes 10M events/sec" or "A self-serve onboarding flow that gets users to value in <5 minutes"

### 5. Management Systems
What processes, metrics, and structures keep the strategy on track?
- How do you measure whether the strategy is working?
- How do you allocate resources to reinforce the strategy?
- How do you kill projects that don't fit?
- What's the cadence for strategy review and adaptation?

## The 9-Section Product Strategy Canvas

Use this canvas to document the complete product strategy. Each section should be 3-8 bullet points of substance, not filler.

### 1. Vision
- How can we inspire people? What are we aspiring to achieve?
- What values do we uphold?
- The vision should make people FEEL something, not just understand direction

**Crafting a strong vision:**
- **Inspiring**: Motivates the team to commit — would someone talented choose to work on this?
- **Achievable**: Realistic given resources, market, and timeline — ambitious but not delusional
- **Emotional**: Creates meaning and connection — not corporate jargon
- **Differentiated**: Could not describe your closest competitor
- **Memorable**: Communicable in one sentence. If you need a paragraph, it's not a vision.

**Vision examples:**
- **Weak**: "Empowering teams to work better together" (could be anyone)
- **Weak**: "The world's most innovative collaboration platform" (says nothing)
- **Strong**: "Every designer ships with confidence, without waiting for engineering" (Webflow)
- **Strong**: "Make the world's information universally accessible and useful" (Google — ambitious but specific)

**Process**: Draft 3-5 variations. Test each against: (1) Does it differentiate? (2) Does it inspire? (3) Does it guide decisions? Select the strongest and explain your rationale.

### 2. Market Segments
- Markets are defined by people's PROBLEMS, not demographics
- Jobs to Be Done (JTBD): what outcome is the customer hiring your product for?
- Desired outcomes, constraints, and context
- Who is the FIRST segment? Why this one first?

**First-segment selection criteria:**
- Acute pain (they're actively spending money/time on the problem)
- Reachable (you know where they are and can get to them)
- Willingness to pay (they have budget and authority)
- Strategic value (winning here creates a path to adjacent segments)
- Serviceable (your current capabilities can deliver for them)

### 3. Relative Costs
- Do you optimize for low cost (Southwest Airlines) or unique value (Starbucks)?
- Low cost does NOT necessarily mean low price
- What is your cost position relative to competitors?
- Where do you invest MORE than competitors? Where do you invest LESS?

### 4. Value Proposition
For each target segment:
- **What before**: The customer's current situation — specific pain, workflow, cost
- **How**: How your product delivers the solution — specific features and capabilities
- **What after**: The improved outcome — measurable change in the customer's life/work
- **Alternatives**: What customers use today. Why they'd switch to you. What switching costs.

### 5. Trade-offs
- What will you NOT do? List specific features, markets, and customer types you're declining.
- How does saying "no" create focus and amplify your value?
- What would a competitor have to sacrifice to copy your integrated set of choices?

**This is the most important section.** A strategy without trade-offs is not a strategy. If everything is a priority, nothing is.

**Examples of real trade-offs:**
- "We won't build an enterprise sales motion. This means we'll lose deals >$100K but keep our product simple and self-serve."
- "We won't support on-premise deployment. This alienates regulated industries but lets us ship weekly and maintain one codebase."

### 6. Key Metrics
- **North Star Metric**: Single metric that captures the core value you deliver to customers. Not revenue — the thing that, if it grows, revenue follows.
- **OMTM (One Metric That Matters)**: The one metric to optimize THIS quarter, derived from your current strategic priority.
- **Input metrics**: 2-3 leading indicators that predict the North Star.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using revenue as North Star (it's a lagging output, not a value indicator)
- Tracking too many metrics (if you have 15 KPIs, you have zero)
- Vanity metrics (total signups without activation or retention)

### 7. Growth
- Sales-Led Growth (SLG) or Product-Led Growth (PLG)? Or hybrid?
- Primary acquisition channels — be specific (not "social media" but "LinkedIn thought leadership targeting VP Product personas")
- How do you scale? What's the growth loop?
- Unit economics: CAC, LTV, payback period, LTV/CAC ratio

### 8. Capabilities
- What competencies and resources do you need to win?
- What do you build vs. buy vs. partner for?
- What capabilities must you develop that you don't have today?
- Where are the capability gaps that could block strategy execution?

### 9. Can't/Won't (Defensibility)
- Why can't competitors easily copy this? Be honest — most "moats" are wishful thinking.
- What defensibility do you ACTUALLY have?

**Real defensibility (ranked by strength):**
1. **Network effects**: Each user makes the product more valuable for others (marketplaces, social platforms)
2. **Switching costs**: Customers have invested time/data/integrations that make leaving expensive
3. **Data advantages**: You have proprietary data that improves your product and competitors can't access
4. **Economies of scale**: Your unit costs decrease with volume in ways competitors can't match
5. **Regulatory/legal barriers**: Licenses, patents, regulatory approvals that take years
6. **Brand**: Strong brand in a specific category (takes years to build, hard to replicate)

**Not real defensibility:**
- "Our team" (people leave; competitors hire)
- "First mover advantage" (rarely durable — Google wasn't the first search engine)
- "Our technology" (unless patented, it can be rebuilt)
- "Our culture" (not a structural barrier)

## Strategic Narrative: Why Now?

Every strategy needs a "why now" narrative. What changed in the world that creates this opportunity? Without it, the strategy lacks urgency and conviction.

Three types of "why now" triggers:
1. **Technology enabler**: A new technology makes something possible that wasn't before (e.g., LLMs enabling natural-language interfaces)
2. **Regulatory shift**: A law or regulation creates new requirements or removes old barriers (e.g., GDPR creating demand for privacy tools)
3. **Behavioral change**: People's habits or expectations shifted (e.g., remote work normalizing async collaboration)

If you can't articulate why now, ask: is this actually a timely opportunity, or a solution looking for urgency?

## The Startup Canvas

For NEW products or early-stage ventures, extend the 9-section canvas with:

### 10. Cost Structure
- Fixed vs. variable costs
- Key cost drivers and how they scale
- Burn rate and runway implications

### 11. Revenue Streams
- Revenue model: subscription, usage, transaction, licensing
- Pricing approach: penetration, value-based, competitive
- Revenue scalability and biggest uncertainties

**When to use which canvas:**
- **Product Strategy Canvas (9 sections)**: Established products with market presence
- **Startup Canvas (11 sections)**: New products needing both strategy AND business model clarity
- **Business Model Canvas**: When you need to communicate operational mechanics to investors or corporate stakeholders (see business-models skill)
- **Lean Canvas**: Quick hypothesis testing when speed matters more than depth (see business-models skill)

## Validating Strategic Hypotheses

Every strategy rests on assumptions. Make them explicit and test them.

### Hypothesis Format
```
We believe [target segment] will [behavior/action]
because [reason/evidence].
We'll know this is true when [measurable signal].
We'll test this by [experiment] within [timeframe].
```

### Prioritize by Risk
Plot each hypothesis on: **Impact if Wrong** (high/low) x **Confidence** (high/low)
- High impact, low confidence = TEST IMMEDIATELY
- High impact, high confidence = Monitor
- Low impact, low confidence = Ignore
- Low impact, high confidence = Ignore

### Minimum Viable Tests
- **Demand**: Landing page with signup, ad campaigns measuring click-through
- **Willingness to pay**: Price on the landing page, founder-led sales conversations
- **Problem severity**: Customer interviews (do they mention this problem unprompted?)
- **Solution fit**: Prototype testing, concierge MVP, Wizard of Oz

## Common Strategy Failure Modes

1. **The "Everything for Everyone" strategy**: No trade-offs, no focus. Symptom: feature list looks like every competitor combined.
2. **The "Build It and They Will Come" strategy**: No growth plan. Assumes product quality alone creates demand.
3. **The "Fast Follower" strategy**: Copy the leader's features. Problem: you're always behind, and you haven't explained why customers would switch.
4. **The "Technology-First" strategy**: Lead with technical capability instead of customer problem. "We use AI" is not a strategy.
5. **The "Underpants Gnome" strategy**: Step 1: Build product. Step 2: ???. Step 3: Profit. Missing the theory of how value creation leads to value capture.
6. **The "Pivot-Ready" strategy**: So flexible it commits to nothing. "We'll figure it out based on data." You need a starting hypothesis to generate data.
7. **The "Consensus" strategy**: Tries to make every stakeholder happy. Result: a muddled document that offends no one and inspires no one.
