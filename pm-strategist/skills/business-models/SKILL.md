# Business Models

## What Is a Business Model?

A business model describes how you **create, deliver, and capture value**. It is NOT the same as:
- **Revenue model**: How you charge (subscription, usage, transaction). This is ONE component.
- **Strategy**: Why you'll win. Business model is the mechanism; strategy is the direction.
- **Financial model**: A spreadsheet projecting revenue and costs. Business model is the logic; financial model is the math.

**Common trap**: Teams confuse "business model" with "how we make money." A business model also covers how you create value (capabilities, partners, activities) and how you deliver it (channels, relationships). Revenue capture is the last step, not the whole model.

---

## Framework Selection Guide

| Situation | Best Canvas | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Established product, corporate stakeholders | Business Model Canvas | Comprehensive, widely understood, good for investor/board communication |
| New startup needing strategic + financial clarity | Startup Canvas (see product-strategy skill) | Separates strategy from business model, addresses vision/tradeoffs/defensibility |
| Quick hypothesis testing, early exploration | Lean Canvas | Fast, problem-focused, hypothesis-driven |
| Exploring monetization for an existing product | Monetization Strategy | Compares multiple revenue models with validation experiments |

## Part 1: Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder)

Nine building blocks organized into value creation (left), value proposition (center), value delivery (right), and financial viability (bottom).

### The 9 Blocks

#### 1. Customer Segments
Who are you creating value for? Be specific.
- Mass market, niche, segmented, multi-sided platform, diversified
- Define by JTBD, not demographics
- For multi-sided platforms, define EACH side separately (e.g., Airbnb: hosts AND guests)

#### 2. Value Propositions
What value do you deliver to each segment?
- Quantitative: price, speed, risk reduction, cost savings
- Qualitative: design, brand/status, convenience, customization
- For each segment, state the value proposition differently — one-size-fits-all means you haven't segmented properly

#### 3. Channels
How do customers discover, evaluate, buy, receive, and get support?
- **Awareness**: How do they learn you exist? (content marketing, ads, word of mouth, partnerships)
- **Evaluation**: How do they decide to buy? (free trial, demo, reviews, case studies)
- **Purchase**: How do they transact? (self-serve, sales call, marketplace)
- **Delivery**: How do they receive value? (cloud, download, physical)
- **After-sales**: How do you support them? (docs, community, support team)

Each channel has a cost. Map channels to CAC.

#### 4. Customer Relationships
What type of relationship does each segment expect?
- Personal assistance (high-touch, expensive, high retention)
- Self-service (low-touch, scalable, higher churn risk)
- Automated (personalized at scale — recommendation engines, drip campaigns)
- Communities (customers help each other — reduces support cost, increases switching cost)
- Co-creation (customers contribute to the product — user-generated content, plugins)

#### 5. Revenue Streams
How does each segment pay? How much?
- Asset sale, subscription, usage fee, licensing, brokerage fee, advertising
- Fixed pricing vs. dynamic pricing vs. negotiated pricing
- Key metric: what is the revenue per customer per year?

#### 6. Key Resources
What assets are essential to make this model work?
- Physical (servers, offices, hardware)
- Intellectual (patents, brand, proprietary data, algorithms)
- Human (key talent, specialized skills)
- Financial (cash reserves, credit lines, revenue)

#### 7. Key Activities
What must you do exceptionally well?
- Production (building the product)
- Problem-solving (consulting, custom solutions)
- Platform/network management (matching supply and demand)

#### 8. Key Partnerships
Who helps you that you couldn't do alone?
- Strategic alliances (non-competitors)
- Joint ventures (creating new business together)
- Buyer-supplier (reliable supply chain)
- Ecosystem partnerships (platform integrations)

#### 9. Cost Structure
What are the most significant costs?
- Fixed costs: salaries, rent, licenses (don't scale with usage)
- Variable costs: hosting, transaction fees, support (scale with usage)
- **Is this cost-driven** (minimize cost, e.g., Ryanair) or **value-driven** (premium value, e.g., Apple)?

### BMC Honest Critique

The Business Model Canvas has real limitations you should know:

- **No vision**: It doesn't ask WHY — what purpose drives the team?
- **No trade-offs**: It asks what you DO, never what you WON'T do. Without trade-offs, there's no focus.
- **No defensibility analysis**: "Key Resources" is a weak substitute for analyzing why competitors can't copy your integrated model.
- **No metrics**: There's no section for how you'll know the model is working.
- **Key Partnerships and Key Resources are often filler** for startups: "cloud provider" and "engineering team" appear on every startup's canvas.

**When BMC works best**: Communicating an established business model to investors, board members, or corporate stakeholders who expect this format. It provides a complete operational picture.

**When BMC fails**: Early-stage products that need strategic clarity, not operational documentation. Use the Startup Canvas (product-strategy skill) instead.

## Part 2: Lean Canvas (Ash Maurya)

Replaces Partners, Activities, and Resources with Problem, Solution, and Unfair Advantage. Optimized for speed and hypothesis testing.

### The 9 Blocks (Lean Canvas)

| # | Block | Question |
|---|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Problem** | Top 3 problems your customers have |
| 2 | **Customer Segments** | Target customers and early adopters |
| 3 | **Unique Value Proposition** | Single, clear, compelling message |
| 4 | **Solution** | Top 3 features that address the problems |
| 5 | **Channels** | How you reach customers |
| 6 | **Revenue Streams** | Revenue model and pricing |
| 7 | **Cost Structure** | Key costs |
| 8 | **Key Metrics** | Activities you measure |
| 9 | **Unfair Advantage** | What can't be easily copied or bought |

### Lean Canvas Honest Critique

- **Redundancy**: "Problem" overlaps with Customer Segments (markets ARE defined by problems). "Solution" overlaps with UVP. This creates confusion.
- **Missing sections**: No vision, no trade-offs, no relative cost positioning, no growth strategy.
- **"Unfair Advantage" is too narrow**: Defensibility should come from the INTEGRATED set of choices, not a single element.
- **No coherence check**: Doesn't ask whether all elements reinforce each other.

**When Lean Canvas works best**: 20-minute brainstorming sessions. Quick hypothesis generation. Comparing multiple ideas side-by-side.

**When Lean Canvas fails**: When you need strategic depth. It's a napkin sketch, not a strategy document.

## Part 3: Monetization Strategy

When exploring HOW to make money, evaluate multiple approaches before committing.

### Monetization Models Reference

| Model | How It Works | Best For | Key Risk | Key Metric |
|-------|-------------|----------|----------|------------|
| **Freemium** | Free base + paid premium | High-volume, low-touch (Spotify, Notion) | Low conversion (1-5% typical) | Free-to-paid conversion rate |
| **Subscription** | Recurring charge for access | Continuous-value products (SaaS) | Churn | Net revenue retention (NRR) |
| **Usage-based** | Pay per consumption | APIs, infrastructure, variable-need products | Unpredictable revenue | Revenue per unit of consumption |
| **Per-seat** | Price per user | Team collaboration tools | "Seat compression" (sharing logins) | Revenue per seat, expansion rate |
| **One-time purchase** | Single payment | Tools, templates, niche software | No recurring revenue | Units sold, repeat purchase rate |
| **Marketplace/transaction** | % of transactions | Platforms connecting supply/demand | Cold-start problem | GMV, take rate |
| **Advertising** | Revenue from ads | High-traffic consumer products | UX degradation, ad-blocker risk | RPM, user engagement |
| **Enterprise license** | Annual contract, custom pricing | Complex B2B with high switching costs | Long sales cycles, implementation overhead | ACV, win rate |

### For Each Candidate Model, Evaluate:

1. **Fit with customer JTBD**: Does the pricing model align with how customers get value? (Usage-based makes sense when value scales with usage. Per-seat makes sense when value scales with team adoption.)
2. **Unit economics**: Estimate CAC, LTV, payback period, gross margin. Is this viable?
3. **Competitive positioning**: How do competitors charge? Is there an opportunity to differentiate on pricing model (not just price)?
4. **Scalability**: Does revenue scale with the business, or does it hit a ceiling?
5. **Customer psychology**: Will customers feel they're getting fair value? Usage-based creates cost anxiety. Per-seat penalizes adoption.

### Monetization Validation Experiments

Don't theorize about willingness to pay — TEST it.

| Method | What It Tests | Cost/Effort | Timeline |
|--------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| **Pricing page A/B test** | Price sensitivity, tier preference | Low | 2-4 weeks |
| **Founder sales calls** | WTP at specific price points, objections | Low | 1-2 weeks |
| **Van Westendorp survey** | Price range acceptance | Low | 1 week |
| **Fake door test** | Demand for premium features | Low | 1-2 weeks |
| **Pilot with real pricing** | Actual conversion at actual price | Medium | 4-8 weeks |
| **Cohort analysis** | Price impact on retention and expansion | Medium | 3-6 months |

### Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter

Ask target customers four questions about your product:
1. At what price would it be **so cheap** you'd question its quality?
2. At what price is it a **good deal** — a bargain?
3. At what price is it **getting expensive** — you'd think twice?
4. At what price is it **too expensive** — you'd never buy it?

Plot the responses. The intersection points define:
- **Point of marginal cheapness**: Below this, you lose credibility
- **Point of marginal expensiveness**: Above this, you lose customers
- **Optimal price point**: Maximum revenue per unit
- **Indifference price point**: Equal number of "cheap" and "expensive" responses

## Common Business Model Traps

1. **"We'll monetize later."** If you don't understand how you'll capture value, you don't have a business model. Delaying monetization doesn't make pricing decisions easier — it makes them harder (users expect free).

2. **"Our data is the product."** Data-as-revenue only works when you have (a) unique data competitors can't get, (b) a buyer willing to pay for it, and (c) no privacy/regulatory barriers. Most startups have none of these.

3. **"We'll add a marketplace."** Marketplaces require solving the cold-start problem on BOTH sides simultaneously. This is a business model, not a feature.

4. **"Revenue = business model."** Subscription revenue is not a business model. The model includes how you acquire customers (CAC), how you deliver value (cost of goods/service), how you retain them (retention mechanisms), and THEN how you charge.

5. **"Freemium always works."** Freemium works when (a) marginal cost of free users is near zero, (b) free users create value for paid users (network effects, content), or (c) free usage naturally leads to paid needs (storage limits, team features). If none of these apply, freemium just burns cash.

6. **"We'll figure out pricing in beta."** Beta users are not representative of willingness to pay. They're early adopters who tolerate rough edges — their price sensitivity is different from the mainstream market.

## Business Model Viability Check

Before committing to a business model, verify:

- [ ] LTV > 3x CAC (minimum for sustainability)
- [ ] Payback period < 18 months (for venture-backed) or < 12 months (for bootstrapped)
- [ ] Gross margin > 60% (for SaaS) or > 40% (for marketplace/transaction)
- [ ] Revenue model aligns with how customers perceive value
- [ ] Cost structure scales favorably (variable costs don't grow faster than revenue)
- [ ] At least one monetization hypothesis has been validated with real customers
- [ ] The model works at 10x current scale (not just current size)
