# Positioning

## What Is Positioning?

Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how your product is the best in the world at delivering something a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.

**It is NOT:**
- A tagline (that's copywriting)
- A mission statement (that's internal motivation)
- A feature list (that's a spec sheet)
- Marketing messaging (positioning INFORMS messaging, it is not messaging itself)

**The test**: Read your positioning statement. Replace your product name with your closest competitor's name. If the statement still works, your positioning is too generic.

## Part 1: Value Proposition (6-Part JTBD Template)

This template produces a clear, actionable value proposition. It's designed for one segment per pass — do NOT try to capture multiple segments in one value proposition.

### The 6 Parts

#### 1. WHO
- Who specifically is this for? (Role, company type, situation — not demographics)
- What characterizes them? What are their constraints?
- **Weak**: "Marketing teams"
- **Strong**: "Content marketers at B2B SaaS companies (50-200 employees) who publish 5+ blog posts per week without a dedicated SEO specialist"

#### 2. WHY (Problem / JTBD)
- What's the core job they're trying to get done?
- What outcome are they trying to achieve?
- What's the underlying motivation? (functional, emotional, social)
- **Weak**: "They need better content"
- **Strong**: "They need to consistently produce SEO-optimized content that drives organic traffic, but they don't have SEO expertise and spend 2+ hours per article on keyword research they're not confident in"

#### 3. WHAT BEFORE (Current State)
- What are they using today to solve this problem?
- What does their current workflow look like?
- What's broken, painful, or inefficient?
- **Weak**: "They use various tools"
- **Strong**: "They use a combination of Google Keyword Planner (free but shallow), Ahrefs (powerful but $99/mo and requires SEO knowledge), and gut instinct. The result: inconsistent keyword targeting, 60% of posts never rank on page 1, and 8+ hours per week spent on SEO tasks instead of writing"

#### 4. HOW (Solution)
- How does your product specifically solve this problem?
- What features and capabilities deliver the value?
- What's the mechanism of action? (Not just "AI-powered" — what does the AI actually DO?)
- **Weak**: "AI-powered content optimization"
- **Strong**: "Analyzes top-ranking content for any keyword, generates a content brief with target keywords, headers, and word count, then scores your draft in real-time as you write — no SEO expertise required"

#### 5. WHAT AFTER (Outcome)
- What's the measurable improvement?
- How does their life/work change?
- What becomes possible that wasn't before?
- **Weak**: "Better content performance"
- **Strong**: "Articles rank on page 1 within 60 days (vs. 6+ months). SEO research time drops from 2 hours to 10 minutes per article. Organic traffic grows 3-4x within 6 months without hiring an SEO specialist."

#### 6. ALTERNATIVES (Competitive Context)
- What other solutions could they choose?
- Why would they choose YOU over each alternative?
- What's the switching cost or friction?

| Alternative | Their Strength | Their Weakness | Our Advantage |
|------------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Ahrefs | Deep data, industry standard | Requires SEO expertise, $99+/mo | No expertise needed, 5x faster |
| Hiring an SEO specialist | Dedicated expertise | $60-80K/yr, still needs tools | 1/100th the cost, instant availability |
| Google Keyword Planner | Free, Google data | Surface-level, no content guidance | Full workflow from research to scoring |
| Doing nothing | Zero cost | Content doesn't rank, traffic stagnates | Measurable traffic growth within 60 days |

### Value Proposition Statement

Synthesize the 6 parts into a single statement:

**Template**: "For [WHO] who [WHY/problem], [Product] is the [category] that [HOW/key differentiator]. Unlike [primary alternative], we [WHAT AFTER/key outcome]."

**Example**: "For content marketers at growing B2B SaaS companies who need to drive organic traffic without SEO expertise, ContentRank is the content optimization platform that turns any writer into an SEO expert. Unlike Ahrefs, we require zero SEO knowledge and produce page-1 rankings in 60 days."

### Honest Critique of Strategyzer's Value Proposition Canvas

The Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder) is widely taught but has structural issues:

1. **Product-first layout**: The canvas places the product on the left side, which psychologically leads teams to start with their solution rather than the customer's problem. This 6-part template starts with WHO and WHY.
2. **Multi-segment confusion**: Strategyzer encourages mapping multiple products/services simultaneously, which dilutes focus. This template is one-segment-per-pass by design.
3. **No explicit alternatives section**: Strategyzer doesn't force you to confront what customers would use without you. Section 6 (Alternatives) in this template forces competitive honesty.
4. **Confusing structure**: Separating Customer Jobs, Pains, and Gains on one side and Pain Relievers, Gain Creators, and Products on the other creates confusion about where things go. "What before -> How -> What after" is clearer.
5. **No usable output**: Strategyzer produces a canvas. This template produces a value proposition STATEMENT that marketing, sales, and product can use immediately.

**When to use Strategyzer VPC**: When you have a mature, complex product with many customer segments and need a detailed pains/gains decomposition. It works for analysis, not for communication.

## Part 2: April Dunford's Positioning Framework ("Obviously Awesome")

This is the most practical positioning framework available. It works from the inside out: start with what you HAVE (not what you wish you had), and derive positioning from real strengths.

### The 5 Components (in order)

#### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
What would customers use if your product didn't exist? NOT your direct competitors — what would they ACTUALLY do?

This is often surprising:
- They might use a spreadsheet (not a competing product)
- They might hire a person (not buy software)
- They might do nothing (not switch to a substitute)

List all realistic alternatives. Be honest — if "doing nothing" is the main competitor, that tells you something important about urgency.

#### Step 2: Unique Attributes
What do you have that the alternatives DON'T? These must be objectively true and verifiable — not "better UX" (subjective) but "processes 10x faster" (measurable) or "only solution with native Salesforce integration" (verifiable).

For each attribute:
- Is this unique to us, or do 3 competitors also have it?
- Can a customer verify this independently?
- Does this matter to the target customer, or only to us?

#### Step 3: Value (The "So What?")
Each unique attribute must map to a VALUE that the customer cares about.

| Unique Attribute | So What? (Value to Customer) |
|-----------------|------------------------------|
| Native Salesforce integration | No data sync issues, save 5 hrs/week on manual data entry |
| Real-time collaboration | Team ships 2x faster because reviews happen in-tool, not via email |
| Usage-based pricing | Pay only for what you use — no shelfware, 40% lower cost at current scale |

If you can't connect an attribute to a customer value, it's a feature, not a differentiator.

#### Step 4: Target Customer Characteristics
Who cares A LOT about the value you deliver? These are the customers for whom your unique attributes create the most value.

Characteristics to identify:
- What's their situation that makes your differentiation matter?
- What have they tried that didn't work?
- What are they spending (money/time) on this problem today?
- How urgent is this for them?

**Key insight**: Target customers are not everyone in your segment. They're the subset who disproportionately value YOUR specific differentiation.

#### Step 5: Market Category
What market frame do you place your product in? This sets customer expectations for pricing, features, and competitors.

Three positioning strategies:

| Strategy | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Head-to-head** | You can win on the established criteria | "A project management tool that's faster than Asana" |
| **Subcategory** | You serve a distinct niche within an established category | "Project management built specifically for creative agencies" |
| **New category** | You're creating a fundamentally new way to solve the problem | "Revenue intelligence platform" (Gong — didn't exist before them) |

**Warning about new categories**: Creating a new category is the hardest positioning strategy. You must educate the market about what the category IS before you can sell within it. Only do this when existing categories genuinely can't contain what you do.

### Positioning Statement (Dunford format)

```
[Product] is a [market category]
for [target customers with these characteristics]
who [have this problem/need].

Unlike [competitive alternatives],
[Product] [unique attributes that deliver specific value].
```

## Part 3: Pricing Strategy

Pricing is positioning expressed in dollars. Your price communicates what you are more than any tagline.

### Pricing Models (Expanded)

| Model | Signal to Customer | Best For |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| **Free** | "We're building audience first" | Pre-revenue, network-effect products |
| **Freemium** | "Try before you buy" | Products where free usage leads to paid needs |
| **Low flat rate** | "Simple, predictable, no surprises" | Simple tools, prosumer products |
| **Per-seat** | "Value scales with team size" | Collaboration tools |
| **Usage-based** | "Pay only for what you use" | APIs, infrastructure, variable-consumption products |
| **Tiered** | "We serve different needs at different levels" | Products with distinct use cases by segment |
| **Value-based** | "We price on the value we create, not our cost" | High-impact enterprise tools |
| **Enterprise custom** | "We're serious, high-touch, expensive" | Complex B2B with long sales cycles |

### Competitive Pricing Analysis

Use WebSearch to build this table for your top 3-5 competitors:

```
| Competitor | Free Tier | Basic | Pro | Enterprise | Pricing Model | Value Metric |
|-----------|-----------|-------|-----|------------|---------------|-------------|
```

Then identify:
- **Pricing gaps**: Is there a segment willing to pay $X but no option exists between $free and $expensive?
- **Model opportunity**: Are all competitors per-seat when usage-based would be more fair?
- **Anchor effect**: Is there a dominant price point that sets customer expectations?

### Willingness-to-Pay Assessment

Methods (in order of reliability):

1. **Actual purchase behavior** (most reliable): What do customers currently pay for alternatives?
2. **Sales conversation pricing tests**: Quote different prices to different prospects. Track close rates.
3. **Van Westendorp survey**: 4-question price sensitivity meter (see business-models skill for details).
4. **Conjoint analysis**: Show feature/price bundles, measure preferences. Expensive but rigorous.
5. **Competitive anchoring** (least reliable): Use competitor pricing as a reference point.

### Price Elasticity Signals

- **Inelastic demand** (price increases don't lose many customers): Few alternatives, high switching costs, the product is a small % of total budget, it's a must-have (not nice-to-have)
- **Elastic demand** (price increases lose customers): Many alternatives, low switching costs, the product is a large budget item, it's discretionary

### Pricing Anti-Patterns

1. **Cost-plus pricing**: Adding a margin to your costs. Ignores value to customer. Enterprise software worth $100K/year in productivity gains shouldn't be priced at cost-plus-30%.
2. **Competitor-matching**: Pricing exactly like competitors. You have different positioning — price should reflect it.
3. **Charging too little**: Underpricing signals low quality and makes the business unsustainable. If customers close instantly without negotiation, you're probably too cheap.
4. **Complex pricing**: If customers can't understand what they'll pay without a sales call, you've added unnecessary friction (unless you're deliberately sales-led enterprise).
5. **Pricing on features instead of value**: "Pro plan adds custom reports" is feature gating. "Pro plan for teams that need to share insights with stakeholders" is value gating. Same feature, different framing.

## Part 4: Market Timing — Why Now?

Every strong positioning includes a "why now" that explains what changed to create this opportunity.

### Technology Enablers
A new technology makes something possible or affordable that wasn't before.
- **Example**: LLMs enabling natural-language code generation (GitHub Copilot). The need for developer productivity always existed. The technology to deliver it at scale didn't.
- **Test**: Could this product have existed 3 years ago? If yes, "technology enabler" is not your "why now."

### Regulatory Shifts
New laws create new requirements or remove old barriers.
- **Example**: GDPR created a market for privacy compliance tools. SOC 2 requirements created a market for security compliance automation.
- **Test**: Is there a specific regulation with a specific compliance deadline? If the regulatory landscape hasn't actually changed recently, this is a stretch.

### Behavioral Changes
People's habits, expectations, or circumstances shifted.
- **Example**: Remote work normalizing asynchronous collaboration created demand for tools like Loom, Notion, and Linear. The pandemic accelerated adoption of telehealth.
- **Test**: Can you point to a specific, measurable behavior change in the last 2-3 years? "People are increasingly digital" is not specific enough.

### Market Structure Changes
Industry consolidation, platform shifts, or ecosystem changes.
- **Example**: Twitter/X's instability creating an opening for alternatives (Bluesky, Threads). Google's algorithm updates disrupting existing SEO strategies.
- **Test**: Has a specific event or trend disrupted the existing market structure?

### If You Don't Have a "Why Now"

Ask yourself honestly:
- Is this a timely opportunity, or am I starting a company around a problem I personally find interesting?
- If nobody has solved this despite it being technically possible for years, what's the actual barrier? Is that barrier now gone?
- Could I have started this 3 years ago and gotten the same result? If yes, why didn't someone else succeed already?

Not having a clear "why now" doesn't mean the opportunity is bad — but it means you need a different explanation for why you'll win. Maybe it's a better execution story, or a different distribution approach, or a specific insight about the customer that nobody else has.

## The "Could This Describe Any Competitor?" Test

Apply this test to every piece of positioning you produce:

1. **Read the positioning statement out loud.**
2. **Replace your product name with your top 3 competitors' names.**
3. **If it still works for any of them, it's too generic.**

**Fails the test:**
- "We help teams work more efficiently with powerful collaboration features." (Describes Slack, Notion, Asana, Monday, Teams, and 500 other products.)
- "Our AI-powered platform delivers insights that drive growth." (Describes every analytics and AI tool.)
- "We're the easiest solution for modern teams." (Everyone claims this.)

**Passes the test:**
- "We're the only SEO tool that generates content briefs AND scores your draft in real-time, designed for writers who aren't SEO experts." (Specific workflow, specific user, specific differentiation.)
- "We're a revenue intelligence platform that records and analyzes every sales call, so managers can coach reps on what top performers actually say." (Specific mechanism, specific user, specific value — this is Gong's positioning.)
- "We automate SOC 2 compliance for cloud-native startups in weeks instead of months, by continuously monitoring your AWS/GCP infrastructure." (Specific compliance, specific customer, specific mechanism, specific platform.)

## Positioning Worksheet Summary

When building positioning from scratch, work through this checklist in order:

1. [ ] List all competitive alternatives (including "do nothing" and "use a spreadsheet")
2. [ ] List your unique, verifiable attributes (things competitors DON'T have)
3. [ ] Map each attribute to customer value (the "so what")
4. [ ] Define target customer characteristics (who cares MOST about your value)
5. [ ] Choose market category (head-to-head, subcategory, or new category)
6. [ ] Write value proposition using 6-part JTBD template
7. [ ] Write positioning statement using Dunford format
8. [ ] Apply "could this describe any competitor?" test
9. [ ] Define pricing that reinforces positioning
10. [ ] Articulate "why now" — what changed to make this timely
