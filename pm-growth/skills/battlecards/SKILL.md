# Competitive Battlecards

## Overview

A battlecard is a concise, sales-ready reference document that helps your team win competitive deals. It is NOT a full competitive analysis (that belongs in `competitive-landscape.md`). A battlecard is what a sales rep pulls up during a call when the prospect says "we are also looking at [Competitor]."

A good battlecard is one page, scannable in 30 seconds, and gives the rep exactly what they need: what to say, what to ask, and what traps to avoid.

## When to Use

- Prospect mentions a specific competitor during a sales conversation
- Preparing for a competitive evaluation or bake-off
- Training new sales reps on competitive positioning
- Updating competitive intelligence after a competitor's product launch or pricing change
- Analyzing win/loss patterns to improve competitive win rate

## Battlecard Sections

### 1. Overview

Quick reference header — the rep should be able to orient themselves in 5 seconds.

```
## Battlecard: [Your Product] vs [Competitor]
Last Updated: [date]
Use When: [situation — e.g., "Mid-market SaaS companies evaluating project management tools"]
```

**Our Story** (2-3 sentences): How WE describe what we do and why it matters to the buyer.
**Their Story** (2-3 sentences): How THEY describe what they do. Use their own language from their website/marketing.

### 2. Quick Comparison Table

Feature-by-feature comparison. Be honest — if they win on a dimension, say so. Credibility with the rep matters more than cheerleading.

| Capability | Us | Them | Verdict |
|-----------|-----|------|---------|
| [Core feature 1] | [Our approach + specifics] | [Their approach + specifics] | Us / Them / Tie |
| [Core feature 2] | ... | ... | ... |
| Pricing (entry) | $X/user/mo | $Y/user/mo | ... |
| Pricing (enterprise) | $X/user/mo | $Y/user/mo | ... |
| Free tier | [details] | [details] | ... |
| Implementation time | [days/weeks] | [days/weeks] | ... |
| Customer support | [channels, SLA] | [channels, SLA] | ... |
| Integrations | [count, key ones] | [count, key ones] | ... |
| Security / Compliance | [SOC2, GDPR, etc.] | [SOC2, GDPR, etc.] | ... |

**Rules for the comparison table**:
- Use specifics, not adjectives. "3-click setup, avg 15 minutes" beats "Easy to set up"
- If you do not know their capability, mark it "[unverified]" — do not guess
- Include pricing WITH the model (per user, per seat, usage-based, flat rate)

### 3. Win Themes

The 3-5 reasons we win when we win. Each must have a proof point.

**Strong example**:
> **Time to value**: Our average customer is live in 3 days vs their 3-week implementation. Proof: Case study — Acme Corp went from signup to first automated workflow in 48 hours.

**Weak example**:
> **Better product**: We have a better, more intuitive product that customers love.

The difference: the strong example gives the rep something concrete to say. The weak example gives them nothing.

### 4. Where They Win (and how to counter)

Honesty builds trust with reps AND with prospects. If a competitor genuinely has an advantage, acknowledge it and provide a counter-position.

**Strong example**:
> **Enterprise integrations**: They have 500+ pre-built integrations vs our 120. **Counter**: Our top 50 integrations cover 94% of customer use cases. Ask the prospect which integrations they need — in most cases, we have them. For gaps, our API + Zapier connector covers the long tail.

**Weak example**:
> **They have more integrations but quality matters more than quantity.**

The difference: the strong example gives the rep a strategy (ask which integrations they need). The weak example is a platitude.

### 5. Objection Handling

The most common objections reps hear when competing with this specific competitor, and exactly how to respond.

**Response framework: Acknowledge, Reframe, Differentiate, Prove**

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "They are cheaper" | **Acknowledge**: "They do have a lower starting price." **Reframe**: "But the right comparison is total cost of ownership over 12 months." **Differentiate**: "Their base plan excludes [X, Y, Z] which are included in ours — their comparable plan is actually $[amount]." **Prove**: "Here is a TCO comparison we built with [Customer Name]." |
| "They have [feature we lack]" | **Acknowledge**: "That is a real feature they have." **Reframe**: "The question is whether that feature solves your specific need." **Differentiate**: "We solve that problem differently through [approach], which our customers tell us works better because [reason]." **Prove**: "[Customer] evaluated both and chose us specifically because [evidence]." |
| "They are bigger / more established" | **Acknowledge**: "They have been around longer, that is true." **Reframe**: "But you are not buying a company's history — you are buying a solution to [their specific problem]." **Differentiate**: "We are [size] which means [benefit: faster support, more responsive to feedback, no legacy baggage]." **Prove**: "[Customer] switched from them to us and saw [specific improvement]." |

**Anti-pattern**: Never trash the competitor to the prospect. Position on YOUR strengths, not their weaknesses. Reps who bash competitors lose credibility.

### 6. Landmine Questions

Questions the rep should ask the prospect that naturally expose competitor weaknesses. These are the highest-value section of the battlecard.

**Strong examples**:
1. "How important is [capability where we excel] to your workflow? Have you evaluated that in their product?" — Forces prospect to investigate an area where competitor is weak.
2. "What does your implementation timeline look like? Have they shared their typical onboarding process?" — If competitor has slow onboarding, this plants doubt.
3. "How many users will need access? Have you looked at their pricing at that scale?" — If competitor's pricing scales poorly, this surfaces the issue.
4. "What is your data residency requirement? Have you confirmed they support [region]?" — If competitor lacks certain compliance, this creates a hard requirement.

**Weak examples**:
1. "Don't you think our product is better?" — Leading, transparent, ineffective.
2. "Are you aware of their security issues?" — Unsubstantiated FUD.

The difference: strong landmines are genuine questions that help the prospect make a better decision. They naturally surface areas where you have an advantage. Weak landmines are transparent sales tactics that damage trust.

### 7. Pricing Comparison

Detailed pricing breakdown, not just headline numbers. Prospects are sophisticated — they will find the details eventually. Be the one who shows them first.

| Tier | Us | Them | Notes |
|------|-----|------|-------|
| Free | [what is included] | [what is included] | [comparison] |
| Starter / Pro | $X/user/mo (annual) | $Y/user/mo (annual) | [what is/is not included] |
| Enterprise | $X/user/mo (annual) | Custom / $Y/user/mo | [key differences in enterprise tier] |
| Overage / usage | [model] | [model] | [hidden costs, metering differences] |
| Implementation | [included / $X] | [$Y / required] | [professional services comparison] |

**Hidden cost analysis**: Document costs that are not on the competitor's pricing page but show up in contracts: implementation fees, training costs, overage charges, API call limits, data export fees, minimum commit periods.

### 8. Customer Proof Points

Specific customer stories that counter this competitor. The more specific, the more credible.

**Strong example**:
> **Acme Corp** (50-person SaaS, Series B): Evaluated both [Us] and [Competitor] for 3 weeks. Chose us because: (1) implementation took 2 days vs estimated 3 weeks with [Competitor], (2) our reporting met their board-level requirements out of the box, (3) their team adopted without training. Quote from VP Ops: "We were up and running before [Competitor's] sales engineer even scheduled the kickoff call."

**Weak example**:
> Customers love our product and many have switched from [Competitor].

### 9. Win/Loss Patterns

Data-driven patterns from actual competitive deals. If you do not have this data yet, start tracking it.

**We tend to win when**:
- Buyer is [role] with [priority] (e.g., "VP Ops who values speed over customization")
- Deal size is [range] (e.g., "20-100 seats")
- Evaluation criteria weight [factor] (e.g., "ease of use is top 2 criteria")

**We tend to lose when**:
- Buyer is [role] with [priority] (e.g., "CTO who requires specific compliance certifications we do not have yet")
- Deal size is [range] (e.g., "500+ seats — they have better enterprise features")
- Evaluation criteria weight [factor] (e.g., "depth of integrations is the deciding factor")

**Key tipping point**: The single factor that most often determines the winner (e.g., "live demo of [specific workflow] — when prospects see our [feature] in action, win rate jumps from 35% to 65%")

## Research Instructions

When building or updating a battlecard, use WebSearch to gather current intelligence:

1. **Pricing**: Search "[competitor] pricing 2026" — pricing pages change frequently. Verify against their actual pricing page (use WebFetch).
2. **Product updates**: Search "[competitor] product launch" or "[competitor] changelog" — recent features can change the competitive dynamic overnight.
3. **Reviews**: Search "site:g2.com [competitor] reviews" and "site:reddit.com [competitor] complaints" — customer reviews reveal real weaknesses that marketing materials hide.
4. **Funding / strategy**: Search "[competitor] funding" or "[competitor] acquisition" — strategic shifts affect positioning and product direction.
5. **Customer sentiment**: Search "[competitor] vs [alternative]" on Reddit, HackerNews, or industry forums — these discussions reveal unfiltered opinions.

**Never fabricate evidence**. If you cannot find a specific data point, mark it as "[needs verification]" and flag it for the user to confirm.

## Battlecard Maintenance

### Update Cadence
- **Quarterly review**: Full refresh of pricing, features, and positioning
- **Triggered update**: Competitor launches a new product, changes pricing, gets acquired, or a major customer publicly switches

### Triggers for Immediate Update
- Competitor changes pricing model (not just price)
- Competitor launches a feature that was a key differentiator for you
- You lose 3+ deals to the same competitor in a quarter with the same reason
- Competitor gets acquired or acquires a company that changes their capabilities
- Major analyst report (Gartner, Forrester) shifts positioning

### Freshness Indicators
- **Green** (< 30 days since update): Current and reliable
- **Yellow** (30-90 days): Likely still accurate but verify pricing and recent launches
- **Red** (90+ days): Must be refreshed before use in a deal

## Output Format

When creating a battlecard, deliver a single markdown document with all nine sections. Keep it scannable:
- Use tables over paragraphs
- Bold key phrases for scanning
- Keep each section to half a page or less
- Total document should fit on 2-3 printed pages maximum

## Anti-Patterns

- **10-page competitive analysis disguised as a battlecard**: Reps will not read it during a call. Keep it to 2-3 pages.
- **All "We Win" and no "They Win"**: Reps will not trust it. Honest acknowledgment of competitor strengths builds credibility.
- **Generic objection responses**: "We are better because of our platform" is not a response. Specific evidence, specific counter-positioning.
- **Outdated pricing**: Nothing destroys credibility faster than citing wrong prices. Verify before every use.
- **No landmines section**: The landmines are the most operationally valuable part. Teach reps what to ASK, not just what to SAY.
- **Claims without sources**: Every competitive claim must have a source (review site, pricing page, customer quote). Unsourced claims are opinions, not intelligence.
